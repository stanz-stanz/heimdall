## Context

Three culturally-aligned pitch frameworks for email outreach to SMB owners. Each uses a different Danish cultural lever to frame the value of EASM in terms that resonate with the target audience.

These are strategic angles to shape email copy, not verbatim scripts. Pair with the templates in `email-and-dm-templates.md`.

---

## Strategy 1: The "Open Window" Analogy

**Cultural key:** Nabohjælp (neighbor-helping-neighbor)

In Denmark, Nabohjælp is about noticing if a neighbor's window is open while they are away.

**The pitch:**

> "I'm not here as a consultant, but as a local who uses your services. I've noticed that many of our local shops have 'digital windows' left wide open — like old booking systems or unsecure payment links. EASM is just a way for me to help you 'close the window' before someone walks in who shouldn't be there."

**When to use:** Service businesses (restaurants, physios, hairdressers) where the neighbor framing removes the consultant-client power dynamic.

---

## Strategy 2: The "Solidarity" Argument

**Cultural key:** Collective responsibility ("we are only as strong as our weakest link")

Danes value the idea that community trust is shared infrastructure.

**The pitch:**

> "When one local business in town gets hacked, it makes everyone nervous about booking online locally. I'm trying to help a few of my favorite spots stay secure so that we keep the trust of the whole community. It's about making sure we don't accidentally hand over our neighbors' data to criminals."

**When to use:** Businesses where community identity is strong — local shopping streets, erhvervsforeninger (business associations), or businesses that rely on local repeat customers. The framing shifts from individual risk to collective benefit (Janteloven-compatible).

---

## Strategy 3: The "Ordentlighed" (Common Decency) Approach

**Cultural key:** Ordentlighed (doing things properly)

Frame it as a "digital health check" rather than a certification.

**The pitch:**

> "You've built a great business here based on trust. I have a tool that can run a quick scan to see if there are any 'blind spots' in your online setup. It's not about a fancy certificate; it's just about doing things ordentligt (properly) for the people who live here."

**When to use:** Established businesses with a reputation to protect — dental clinics, accounting firms, law offices. The "ordentlighed" framing appeals to professional pride without triggering Janteloven (no claim of superiority, just doing the right thing).

---

## Combined Pitch (Danish)

A unified email draft that blends all three cultural levers — Nabohjælp (neighbor), solidarity (collective data trust), and ordentlighed (doing things properly):

> "Jeg er nabo og kommer her tit, så jeg er personligt investeret i, at det går jer godt. Jeg har lagt mærke til, at mange små virksomheder ubevidst har 'åbne døre' i deres bookingsystemer, hvor vores allesammens data ligger.
>
> Jeg vil gerne tilbyde et hurtigt nabotjek af jeres digitale facade for at sikre, at jeres kunders tillid er i gode hænder. Det handler om at have styr på sagerne hele vejen rundt, præcis som I har i resten af forretningen. Skal vi bruge to minutter på at se, om jeres døre er låst?"

## Usage Notes

- All three strategies pair with the "first finding free" hook — include a specific finding from the prospect's brief to make the email concrete
- Read the prospect's brief (`data/output/briefs/{domain}.json`) before writing so you can reference a real finding
- Follow provenance rules: only state confirmed findings as facts; frame unconfirmed findings as "your detected version is known to be affected by..."
